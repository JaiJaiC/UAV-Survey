Emergent Cooperative Strategies for Pursuit-Evasion in Cluttered
Environments: A Knowledge-Enhanced Multi-Agent Deep
Reinforcement Learning Approach
Yihao Sun, Chao Yan, Han Zhou, Xiaojia Xiang and Jie Jiang
Abstract — Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has recently
emerged as a promising tool for tackling pursuit-evasion tasks.
However, most existing DRL-based pursuit approaches still rely
on individual rewards and struggle with complex scenarios. To
address these challenges, we propose a knowledge-enhanced
DRL approach for multi-agent pursuit-evasion in complex
environments. Speciﬁcally, the cooperative pursuit problem
is modeled as a decentralized partially observable Markov
decision process from each pursuers perspective, where the
team reward function is elaborately designed to encourage
collaborative behavior and enhance team coordination. Then,
a novel knowledge enhanced multi-agent twin delayed deep
deterministic policy gradient (KE-MATD3) algorithm is pre-
sented to efﬁciently learn the cooperative pursuit policy. By
integrating a knowledge enhancement mechanism that extracts
effective information from an improved artiﬁcial potential
ﬁeld method, the cooperative pursuit policy achieves more
robust convergence, mitigating the local optima that typically
arise from individual reward-based learning. Finally, extensive
numerical simulations and real-world experiments validate the
efﬁciency and superiority of the proposed approach, demon-
strating emergent cooperative behaviors among the pursuers.
I. INTRODUCTION
In recent years, multi-agent systems (MASs) have demon-
strated signiﬁcant potential in complex and dangerous tasks
such as air combat, target interception, and urban air mo-
bility management [1], [2], [3]. These applications typi-
cally involve multi-agent cooperative pursuit problems. The
objective is to enable each pursuing agent to collaborate
seamlessly with teammates while avoiding conﬂicts and
adapting to dynamic environments, thereby ensuring that the
target can be captured effectively [4].
To enhance the autonomy and adaptability of multi-agent
systems in cooperative pursuit tasks, researchers have de-
voted signiﬁcant efforts [5], [6]. Traditional methods, includ-
ing Hamilton equation solutions [7], model predictive control

To address this limitation, model-free deep reinforcement
learning (DRL) [10], [11], [12] has gained increasing at-
tention as a promising alternative. Various DRL-based ap-
proaches have been proposed to solve cooperative pursuit
problems. For instance, Souza et al. [13] employed cur-
riculum learning to progressively scale the pursuit policy
from simpler to more complex tasks, but did not consider
obstacles. Zhang et al. [14] introduced a CBC-TP Net
framework for multi-UA V pursuit in urban settings, yet
circumvented head-on collision issues by assigning different
ﬂight altitudes. Li et al. [15] adopted a modiﬁed MAPPO
algorithm for multi-USV cooperative pursuit with obsta-
cles, although their settings were constrained to sparsely
distributed and ﬁxed obstacle placements. Likewise, Xu
et al. [16] proposed the MMOTS solution for underwater
multi-AUV pursuit-evasion, though they assumed full state
observability among pursuit AUVs and overlooked potential
perception constraints. Overall, many existing approaches
rely on simpliﬁed assumptions that fail to capture real-world
complexities.
Furthermore, most current DRL-based pursuit approaches
employ individual reward settings [17], [18], [19], [20], [21],
which typically lead agents to prioritize their individual
interests. Approaches could be particularly problematic in
collaborative tasks requiring team coordination, potentially
resulting in suboptimal behavior patterns. For instance, in
trafﬁc management systems, rewarding individual intersec-
tion efﬁciency might exacerbate congestion at adjacent junc-
tions [22]. In contrast, team rewards promote collective
goal consideration, motivating agents to maximize returns
from a team perspective. However, even state-of-the-art RL
algorithms such as MATD3 [23] and MAPPO [24] struggle
to perform effectively when dealing with team rewards due
to the notorious credit assignment problem [25]. While these
algorithms may show some success in simple tasks using
team rewards, such as multi-agent particle environments
(MPE) [26], they often falter as task complexity increases.
To foster collaborative behaviors among perceptually lim-
ited pursuers in complex environments, this paper proposes
a team reward based knowledge-enhanced multi-agent twin
delayed deep deterministic policy gradient (KE-MATD3) al-

III. METHOD
In this section, we introduce the knowledge enhanced
multi-agent twin delayed deep deterministic policy gradient
(KE-MATD3) algorithm for learning effective team strate-
gies in complex pursuit-evasion scenarios. Building on the
improved artiﬁcial potential ﬁeld (IAPF) method, we embed
domain-speciﬁc heuristics into KE-MATD3 through a dedi-
cated knowledge enhancement mechanism, as illustrated in
Fig.1(b). This design leverages a shared team reward to fos-
ter cooperative behaviors and tackle challenges such as credit
assignment and sparse feedback commonly encountered in
multi-agent environments.

IV. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
In this section, we evaluate our KE-MATD3 algorithm in
numerical simulation and real-world experiment.
A. Parameters Settings
We implemented our algorithm using the PyTorch library.
As illustrated in Fig. 1(b), the policy network employed a
three-layer MLP architecture with 256, 256, and 2 neurons in
each layer respectively. For the critic component, we utilized
two separate value networks, each consisting of a four-layer
MLP with 256, 128, 64, and 1 neurons in successive layers.
All network parameters were updated using the Adam opti-
mizer, with learning rates of 5 ×10-4for the actor and 1×10-3
for each critic. During training, the exploration parameter
σwas exponentially annealed from 1.0 to 0.05, and the
weighting coefﬁcient λwas set to 1 to balance learning and
knowledge enhancement.
The proposed algorithm was trained for 3 ×105steps in a
200×200 m square environment with N= 4pursuers and
M= 20 obstacles. The radii of obstacles were randomly
generated values ranging from 5 m to 10 m. Upon target
capture or mission timeout, the environment was reset,
randomizing the initial positions of the target and pursuers, as
well as the locations and sizes of obstacles. We conducted 5
independent training runs with different random seeds for all
experiments. Table Ipresents the empirical values for other
essential parameters.

C. Real-World Experiment Results
We ﬁnally carried out a real-world experiment to evaluate
the practicability of the learned KE-MATD3 policy.
1) Physical Experimental Setup: As illustrated in Fig.
5(a), the real-world system comprises 5 Crazyﬂie 2.1 [33]
quadcopters, a NOKOV motion capture system, 20 cylindri-
cal obstacles, and an onboard computer. Each quadcopter,
measuring 92×92×29 mm, serves as an agent in our ex-
periments and is equipped with an LED module that displays
green for pursuers and red for the target. The motion capture
system tracks 16 mm-diameter markers on the Crazyﬂies,
determines their real-time positions and velocities, and relays
this data to the onboard computer via ROS messages. The
experimental arena measures 6.4 ×11×2 m and contains
cylindrical obstacles with a 20 cm radius and 1 m height. All
quadcopters maintain a ﬁxed altitude of 0.5 m throughout
the experiment. The onboard computer executes the trained
policy network and transmits control commands wirelessly
to the quadcopters at 400 Hz. Due to the arena constraints,the targets is capped at 0.25 m /s, while pursuers are limited
tovmax=0.25/1.5=0.167 m /s.
2) Trajectory and Key Frame Analysis: Fig. 5(b) depicts
the motion trajectories of pursuers and the target, with color
intensity indicating time progression. At the beginning, all
pursuers took off from the right side of the arena and
ascended to 0.5m altitude (Fig. 5(c)-I). Then, they moved
towards the target in a dispersed formation to minimize the
risk of inter-agent collisions (Fig. 5(c)-II). At t=27 s, a
tactical divergence occurred: pursuers 1, 2, and 3 continued
direct pursuit, while pursuer 4 hovered near obstacle B in an
ambush position (Fig. 5(c)-III). This strategy proved effective
att=43 s, when pursuers 1, 2, and 3 forced the target
to circle around obstacle A into the lower left quadrant.
Seizing the opportunity, pursuer 4 immediately sprang into
action, coordinating with the others to intercept the target
near obstacle A (Fig. 5(c)-IV). Ultimately, at t=45 s, the
target was captured by pursuer 4 (Fig. 5(c)-V). This result
demonstrates that the learned KE-MATD3 policy is capable
of safely and efﬁciently completing the capture task while
enabling emergent cooperative behaviors among pursuers.
V. CONCLUSION
This paper introduces KE-MATD3, a novel team reward
based knowledge-enhanced cooperative pursuit algorithm tai-
lored for capture tasks in cluttered environments. By integrat-
ing an improved artiﬁcial potential ﬁeld (IAPF) method with
a knowledge enhancement (KE) mechanism into the MATD3
algorithm, we effectively leverage domain-speciﬁc heuristics
to guide policy learning. Although IAPF alone may not fully
account for all kinematic constraints, the KE mechanism ex-
tracts valuable information from the method, thereby expedit-
ing learning. Numerical simulations illustrate KE-MATD3s
superior learning efﬁciency and performance compared to
various baselines, while approaches that incorporate the KE17144
